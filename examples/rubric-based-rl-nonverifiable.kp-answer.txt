Rubrics as Rewards (RaR) established the foundational approach of replacing verifiable rewards (RLVR) with structured, multi-criterion rubrics graded by an LLM judge as a dense on-policy RL signal for open-ended domains, scoring rollouts against instance-specific criteria aggregated into a single scalar reward and yielding up to 31% relative improvement on HealthBench and 7% on GPQA-Diamond over Likert-based LLM-as-judge baselines, while also improving alignment for smaller judges (arXiv:2507.17746, gunjal2025). The 2026 wave of named systems diverges along three axes. On automatic and self-evolving rubric generation, EvoRubric evolves rubrics during training and uncovers novel discriminative dimensions beyond static expert annotations (arXiv:2605.29847, guan2026); RLCER shows self-proposed, self-evolving rubrics supply reliable chain-of-thought supervision even without outcome rewards (arXiv:2602.10885, sheng2026); AMARIS adds a memory of recurring failures so rubric edits are not driven by purely local batch evidence (arXiv:2605.18592, wu2026); and QUBRIC co-designs queries and rubrics, arguing rubric quality is bottlenecked by query structure (arXiv:2606.03968, zhang2026). On rubric-ensemble and EM-style judge training, RUBRIC-ARROW alternates pointwise rubric reward modeling (arXiv:2605.29156, jiang2026), Open Rubric System (OpenRS) scales a pairwise adaptive rubric reframing reward as inspectable principle reasoning rather than a learned judge function (arXiv:2602.14069, jia2026), and RubricEM treats rubrics as the shared interface structuring policy execution, judge feedback, and agent memory (arXiv:2605.10899, li2026). On rubric-anchored policy optimization, SRaR attributes each rubric item to a reasoning step and normalizes per-step scores so only varying steps yield signal (arXiv:2605.17291, xie2026), POW3R replaces static aggregation with policy-aware weighting that tracks a criterion's current optimization usefulness rather than human-assigned importance (arXiv:2605.20164, tyagi2026), and Shen et al. rethink rubric generation to combat poor coverage, conflated dimensions, and correlated criteria (arXiv:2602.05125, shen2026).

The failure-mode literature is sharp and unresolved. Reward hacking is documented as policy exploitation of latent judge biases that is subtle and entangled (arXiv:2606.04923, wang2026); weak verifiers produce non-transferring proxy gains and stronger verifiers reduce but do not eliminate exploitation (arXiv:2605.12474, mahmoud2026); gradient regularization is proposed as an optimization-side fix beating KL penalties (arXiv:2602.18037, ackermann2026); and self-preference bias persists even with objective rubrics, with judges up to 50% more likely to pass their own failed outputs and ensembling only partially helping (arXiv:2604.06996, pombal2026). These leave open whether self-evolving rubrics can stay fresh without expensive proprietary judges, how to validate rubric-reward fidelity against true human quality absent a standardized protocol, and how to escape lossy scalar aggregation and self-preference contamination simultaneously — the gaps our method targets.
