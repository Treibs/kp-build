## Key contributions

- Systematic review and meta-analysis (PRISMA, QUADAS-2) comparing consumer wearable sleep-tracking devices against laboratory polysomnography in healthy adults; 16 studies included, 10 in the meta-analysis
- Quantified directional bias: wearables overestimated total sleep time and sleep efficiency and underestimated wake after sleep onset, with substantial between-device variability
- Found no device with consistently superior performance; closest PSG agreement was Oura Ring (3rd gen) for sleep latency/efficiency and selected Fitbit models for deep/REM sleep
- Concludes wearables give reasonable global sleep estimates but cannot serve as stand-alone diagnostic tools or for detailed sleep-stage characterization
