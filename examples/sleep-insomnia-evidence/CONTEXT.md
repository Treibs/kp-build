**Scope:** In scope: interventions for improving sleep and treating insomnia in adults — cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I), its components and digital delivery; supplements (melatonin dose/timing, magnesium); light therapy and circadian approaches; pharmacotherapy including newer dual orexin receptor antagonists; and a critical evidence appraisal of popular/contested consumer interventions (mouth-taping, blue-light-blocking glasses, wearable sleep trackers and orthosomnia). Out of scope: detailed OSA surgical/CPAP management, narcolepsy pharmacology, pediatric sleep, and basic sleep neuroscience unconnected to interventions. Emphasis on meta-analyses and RCTs (2016-2026) with an honest evidence-vs-hype framing.

## Open problems (where new work goes)

- **CBT-I is first-line and efficacious, but making it available and accessible at population scale remains unsolved; therapist scarcity drives patients toward medication instead.** (partially-addressed) — Guidelines (ACP) make CBT-I the strong first recommendation, yet inadequate resources mean medications are used more frequently worldwide; digital/internet CBT-I is a proposed but incomplete fix whose effects depend on personal clinical support and are eroded by dropout. *Flagged by [zachariae2016], [qaseem2016], [decrescenzo2022].*
- **Long-term and head-to-head efficacy/safety data for newer insomnia pharmacotherapies (including dual orexin antagonists) are incomplete.** (open) — Network meta-analysis flags that several licensed drugs lack long-term safety information and that for agents such as daridorexant and suvorexant the long-term/tolerability picture is poor or absent; without it, ranking and durable prescribing decisions stay uncertain. *Flagged by [decrescenzo2022], [sateia2017].*
- **Consumer wearable sleep trackers cannot reliably substitute for polysomnography, particularly for detecting wake and characterizing sleep stages.** (open) — Across home and lab validations, devices show high sleep sensitivity but low wake specificity and systematically overestimate total sleep time/efficiency; without solving wake detection and stage classification they cannot serve as stand-alone diagnostic tools, and substitution for actigraphy in insomnia is still unproven. *Flagged by [agostinho2026], [chinoy2022], [kahawage2019], [dezambotti2024].*
- **The quality and consistency of evidence (and of the products themselves) for supplements is too poor to support firm recommendations.** (open) — The magnesium evidence base is rated low to very-low quality with high risk of bias, and melatonin supplements vary wildly from their labels (and can contain serotonin), so even a 'modest effect' cannot be reliably delivered by what consumers actually buy. *Flagged by [mah2021], [erland2017], [ferraciolioda2013].*
- **Individual variability and equity gaps in wearable sensor performance are unresolved.** (open) — Person-specific factors such as skin color and obesity can degrade sensor accuracy, risking augmentation of existing healthcare disparities, and translation of device performance to people with comorbidities is unclear. *Flagged by [dezambotti2024].*
- **The evidence for popular consumer interventions like mouth-taping and blue-light-blocking glasses is thin, underpowered, or null, and adequately powered standardized RCTs are lacking.** (open) — Mouth-taping rests on only a handful of small studies with a potentially serious harm signal, and blue-light-blocking glasses show non-significant actigraphic effects in pooled crossover trials; the gap between social-media-driven adoption and rigorous evidence leaves clinicians unable to advise confidently. *Flagged by [rhee2025], [lunarangel2025], [bigalke2021].*
- **Tracker-driven preoccupation with sleep ('orthosomnia') is an emerging clinical problem that lacks established prevalence data and validated management pathways.** (partially-addressed) — Patients increasingly trust device data over validated measurement and develop perfectionistic sleep anxiety; a measurement scale (BOS) now exists, but how to prevent and treat orthosomnia — and whether trackers can be safely folded into CBT-I — remains underdeveloped. *Flagged by [baron2017], [guldbrandsen2025].*

## Open debates / contested points

- **Does melatonin work as a low-dose physiologic chronobiotic or as a higher-dose hypnotic — and does dose actually matter?**
    - *Low physiologic doses (~0.3 mg) are optimal; high doses are not better and carry side effects* ([zhdanova2001]): A controlled crossover RCT showed 0.3 mg restored sleep efficiency and normalized plasma melatonin, while the 3.0 mg dose left melatonin supraphysiologically elevated into the daytime and induced hypothermia, arguing the chronobiotic low-dose framing is preferable.
    - *Pooled effect is modest regardless, with weak dose dependence and small absolute benefit* ([ferraciolioda2013]): The 19-RCT meta-analysis found only ~7 min latency and ~8 min total-sleep-time gains overall; meta-regression linked higher dose to somewhat greater latency/total-sleep effects (but not sleep quality), and concluded melatonin's absolute benefit is smaller than other hypnotics — so the practical effect is modest whichever dose is used.
    - *Guidelines recommend against melatonin for chronic insomnia* ([sateia2017], [decrescenzo2022]): The AASM pharmacologic guideline recommends against melatonin as a treatment for chronic insomnia (and the network meta-analysis found melatonin showed no overall material benefit), undercutting hype regardless of dose.
- **Do blue-light-blocking glasses actually improve sleep, or only shift a biomarker / subjective perception?**
    - *No significant objective sleep benefit* ([lunarangel2025], [bigalke2021]): A 2025 meta-analysis of crossover RCTs found only a non-significant reduction in sleep onset latency and concluded current RCT evidence does not support significant effects; an individual healthy-adult RCT likewise found no improvement in objective sleep time or quality.
    - *There is a real mechanistic/subjective signal* ([liset2022], [bigalke2021]): Blocking evening blue light produced an earlier onset and rise of melatonin in pregnant women (a genuine circadian effect), and one RCT found a small reduction in self-reported sleep onset — suggesting the mechanism is real even if downstream objective sleep gains are inconsistent.
- **Are consumer sleep trackers useful tools or a source of harm (orthosomnia) — and can they substitute for clinical measurement?**
    - *Useful for global sleep-wake estimates but not diagnostic* ([agostinho2026], [chinoy2022], [kahawage2019], [dezambotti2024]): Validation work finds wearables give reasonable total-sleep estimates and can even exceed traditional actigraphy for PSG-defined sleep, but they overestimate sleep, misclassify wake, and vary by device — so they are best for sleep-wake trends, not staging or diagnosis or substitution for actigraphy.
    - *Tracker data can drive clinically harmful preoccupation* ([baron2017], [guldbrandsen2025]): Patients increasingly seek treatment for self-diagnosed problems based on tracker readouts, trusting devices over validated polysomnography/actigraphy and developing perfectionistic 'orthosomnia' that correlates with sleep effort, dysfunctional beliefs, and anxiety.
- **Should insomnia be treated first with behavioral therapy or with medication — and where do newer orexin antagonists fit?**  *(resolved)*
    - *Behavioral therapy (CBT-I) is first-line; drugs are adjunctive* ([qaseem2016], [sateia2017]): ACP strongly recommends CBT-I as the initial treatment with pharmacotherapy added only via shared decision-making if CBT-I fails, and the AASM frames all drug recommendations as merely WEAK with downgraded evidence — establishing CBT-I's primacy.
    - *Newer dual orexin antagonists are effective drug options with favorable safety, but do not displace first-line behavioral therapy* ([mignot2022], [karppa2020], [decrescenzo2022]): Daridorexant 25-50 mg and lemborexant improved objective and subjective sleep with comparable-to-placebo adverse events and sustained efficacy, and the network meta-analysis ranks lemborexant favorably — yet the same evidence notes drugs are used more because of inadequate resources, not because they are preferred first-line.
- **Is mouth-taping a legitimate sleep/breathing aid or an unsafe social-media fad?**
    - *Insufficient evidence and a real harm signal* ([rhee2025]): A systematic review found only 2 of 10 small studies showed significant improvement in sleep-apnea markers while others showed none, and concluded there is a potentially serious risk of harm (including asphyxiation with nasal obstruction) for people indiscriminately practicing the trend.
    - *Possible benefit in a narrow subgroup* ([rhee2025]): Two of the included studies did show statistically significant improvement in AHI or oxygen desaturations, leaving open the possibility of a limited clinical benefit in carefully selected mouth-breathers without nasal obstruction — though further studies are required to establish it.
