Are consumer sleep trackers useful tools or a source of harm (orthosomnia) — and can they substitute for clinical measurement?

### Useful for global sleep-wake estimates but not diagnostic
Validation work finds wearables give reasonable total-sleep estimates and can even exceed traditional actigraphy for PSG-defined sleep, but they overestimate sleep, misclassify wake, and vary by device — so they are best for sleep-wake trends, not staging or diagnosis or substitution for actigraphy.

*Held by:* [[papers/agostinho2026]], [[papers/chinoy2022]], [[papers/kahawage2019]], [[papers/dezambotti2024]]

### Tracker data can drive clinically harmful preoccupation
Patients increasingly seek treatment for self-diagnosed problems based on tracker readouts, trusting devices over validated polysomnography/actigraphy and developing perfectionistic 'orthosomnia' that correlates with sleep effort, dysfunctional beliefs, and anxiety.

*Held by:* [[papers/baron2017]], [[papers/guldbrandsen2025]]
