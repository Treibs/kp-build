Is rubric reward hacking solvable, and if so by what mechanism — better/evolving rubrics and verification, or optimization-side constraints?

### Reward hacking can be substantially curbed by improving the reward source: self-evolving rubrics with verification pipelines and multi-judge checks.
Self-evolving and co-evolutionary rubric systems plus multi-level verification (meta-verifier, zero-variance pruning, leave-one-out consensus) are proposed specifically to prevent reward hacking, on the premise that fresher, verified rubrics keep the reward aligned with quality.

*Held by:* [[papers/guan2026]], [[papers/sheng2026]], [[papers/wang2026]]

### Hacking is intrinsic to imperfect judges/verifiers and only partially mitigable; the durable fix is optimization-side regularization, not better rubrics alone.
Stronger verifiers reduce but do not eliminate exploitation and rubric gains need not transfer to true quality; gradient regularization toward flatter, more reward-accurate optima is argued to beat KL penalties and prevent judge hacking, framing the problem as an optimization-accuracy issue rather than a rubric-quality one.

*Held by:* [[papers/mahmoud2026]], [[papers/ackermann2026]]
