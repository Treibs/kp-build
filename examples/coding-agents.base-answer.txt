## Unaided baseline (related-work paragraph, from memory)

Autonomous AI coding agents have advanced rapidly since 2023, organized largely around the SWE-bench evaluation line. SWE-bench (Jimenez et al., 2023/ICLR 2024) introduced the now-standard task of resolving real GitHub issues with test-based validation, and early frontier models resolved only a low-single-digit percentage. The dominant response was better "scaffolding": SWE-agent (Yang et al., 2024) argued that an Agent-Computer Interface — purpose-built file editing, navigation, and execution commands — drives most of the gains, and OpenHands/OpenDevin (Wang et al., 2024) became the de facto open agent platform, building on the CodeAct idea (Wang et al., 2024) of using executable code as the agent action space. A counter-current argued that elaborate agency is overrated: Agentless (Xia et al., 2024) showed a fixed localize-repair-validate pipeline beating complex agents at far lower cost, while AutoCodeRover (Zhang et al., 2024) reached competitive resolve rates with AST-aware code search plus spectrum-based fault localization. Plan-and-execute and multi-agent decomposition appeared in MASAI and CodeR (2024). The self-debugging line traces to Self-Debug (Chen et al., 2023) and Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023), which established verbal reflection / execution-feedback loops reused inside agents. Repo-level navigation moved from string matching to structure: AST search (AutoCodeRover), knowledge-graph + MCTS exploration (the RepoUnderstander/LingmaAgent line), and graph-guided localization (LocAgent, 2025). The 2024–2025 frontier shifted toward training rather than prompting — SWE-Gym (executable training environments), SWE-smith (synthetic data by breaking tests), Lingma SWE-GPT (process-centric training), and SWE-RL (rule-based RL on software evolution, reportedly ~41% on Verified). Finally, an evaluation-rigor strand questions leaderboard validity: SWE-bench Multimodal exposed poor cross-language/visual generalization, SWE-Bench Pro targets long-horizon enterprise tasks, and contamination/memorization critiques argue Verified scores partly reflect training-data recall.

I am fairly confident about the existence and identities of SWE-bench, SWE-agent, Agentless, AutoCodeRover, CodeAct, OpenHands, Self-Debug, Reflexion, MASAI, CodeR, SWE-bench Multimodal, SWE-Gym, and SWE-RL. I am less certain about exact arXiv ids — I would not bet on the trailing digits for several of these from memory — and I am least certain about the very recent (late-2025) papers (SWE-Bench Pro, LocAgent, SWE-smith, and the "does SWE-bench test memory" contamination critique), whose ids and even exact titles I may be misremembering or which may postdate my reliable recall.

## Open problems (from memory)
1. Benchmark contamination/memorization may inflate SWE-bench (esp. Verified) scores; decontaminated and held-out/commercial benchmarks are needed to measure real ability.
2. Poor generalization beyond Python single-PR issues — to other languages, visual/UI software, and long-horizon multi-file enterprise tasks.
3. The unresolved scaffolding-vs-training question (and the related agency-vs-simple-pipeline question), plus immature reward/verifier design for RL on code and unstandardized cost-adjusted comparison.
