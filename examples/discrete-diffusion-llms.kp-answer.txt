The frontier of discrete and masked diffusion language models (2024–2026) has rapidly matured from foundational formulations toward credible alternatives to autoregressive (AR) generation. Early discrete-state diffusion (arXiv:2107.03006) and score-entropy formulations (arXiv:2310.16834) established the paradigm, while simplified masked-diffusion objectives (arXiv:2406.07524) made training practical. Scaling efforts then showed these models are competitive: a 1.1B masked diffusion model (MDM) outperforms TinyLlama on most benchmarks (arXiv:2410.18514), LLaDA reaches LLaMA3-8B parity (arXiv:2502.09992), and LLaDA2.0 pushes to 100B parameters (arXiv:2512.15745). Diffusion even surpasses AR in data-constrained regimes (arXiv:2507.15857) and with uniform-state noise on GSM8K (arXiv:2602.15014).

Quality parity remains contested, however. On exact likelihood and perplexity, diffusion LMs still trail AR (arXiv:2406.07524, arXiv:2502.09622), partly because their ELBO is loose and likelihood evaluation is itself unresolved—work like DUEL provides the first exact likelihood for MDMs (arXiv:2603.01367). Some argue MDMs are "secretly" learned-order AR models, their objective a weighted sum of AR losses (arXiv:2511.19152).

Inference speed is the central practical battleground. Bidirectional attention precludes the KV cache, making naive decoding slow (arXiv:2505.15781). Recent methods recover and exceed AR throughput: Fast-dLLM enables training-free KV caching with up to 27.6x speedups (arXiv:2505.22618), Discrete Diffusion Forcing achieves faster-than-AR inference (arXiv:2508.09192). Open problems persist: parallel decoding breaks inter-token dependencies, sampling steps may scale with length (arXiv:2502.09622), and most MDMs remain fixed-length (arXiv:2503.09573, arXiv:2509.01025).
