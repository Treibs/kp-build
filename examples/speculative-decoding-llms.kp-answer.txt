Speculative decoding accelerates autoregressive LLM inference by drafting candidate tokens cheaply and verifying them in parallel against the target model, preserving its output distribution within hardware numerics (arXiv:2302.01318, speculative sampling; surveyed by arXiv:2401.07851). Two families dominate the draft-then-verify frontier. The first is feature-level autoregressive drafting: EAGLE (arXiv:2401.15077) performs autoregression at the second-to-top feature layer rather than the token level, resolving feature-level uncertainty by conditioning on a one-step-advanced token sequence, reaching 2.7x-3.5x lossless speedup on LLaMA2-Chat 70B. Its successors address structural and objective limitations: EAGLE-2 (arXiv:2406.16858) observes that draft-token acceptance is context-dependent, not merely position-dependent, and replaces the static draft tree with a context-aware dynamic one for 3.05x-4.26x speedup; EAGLE-3 (arXiv:2503.01840) abandons feature prediction—which it identifies as the bottleneck limiting data scaling—in favor of direct token prediction with multi-layer feature fusion via training-time test, reaching up to 6.5x. PARD-2 (arXiv:2605.08632) pushes a target-aligned parallel drafter optimizing acceptance length to 6.94x.

A contrasting line minimizes or eliminates the auxiliary drafter. Medusa (arXiv:2401.10774) adds parallel decoding heads to the frozen backbone, verifying multiple candidates per step via tree-based attention (2.2x-3.6x). Lookahead decoding (arXiv:2402.02057) argues draft models are hard to obtain and poorly generalizing, instead using exact Jacobi-style parallel decoding with no auxiliary model (up to 1.8x on MT-bench).

Tree-based multi-candidate verification reshaped the acceptance-vs-overhead trade-off: SpecInfer (arXiv:2305.09781) verifies many candidate continuations simultaneously as a token tree (1.5x-3.5x), and DySpec (arXiv:2410.11744) expands this tree greedily at runtime for higher acceptance.

Critically, these speedups are regime-dependent. Serving analyses report that intra-request speculation competes with batching parallelism and can degrade goodput if added naively (TurboSpec, arXiv:2406.14066); MagicDec (arXiv:2408.11049) counters that addressing the KV bottleneck with a sparse-KV drafter recovers up to 2.51x at batch sizes 32-256, and EAGLE-3 reports 1.38x throughput at batch 64. For long-context and test-time-scaling reasoning workloads, benchmarking shows learned drafters may not justify their cost over cheap n-gram methods (arXiv:2509.04474, arXiv:2408.11049). Acceptance is further brittle under domain shift, motivating alignment-focused drafting (arXiv:2503.07807; arXiv:2505.13204; arXiv:2403.00858) and online-learning drafts exploiting verification feedback (arXiv:2603.12617).
