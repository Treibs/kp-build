## Open problems (where new work goes)

- **Weight loss from incretin pharmacotherapy is not durable off-treatment: stopping semaglutide leads to regain of roughly two-thirds of lost weight within a year, with cardiometabolic gains reverting toward baseline, so it remains unresolved how to maintain benefit without indefinite continuous therapy.** (open) — Obesity is framed as a chronic disease requiring ongoing treatment; durability and exit strategies directly determine real-world effectiveness, adherence burden, and lifetime cost. *Flagged by [wilding2022].*
- **Whether the muscle/lean-mass loss accompanying large GLP-1 RA weight reductions is adaptive or maladaptive (and in whom it becomes clinically harmful, e.g. older or prefrail patients) remains unsettled, and standardized comprehensive muscle-health assessment is lacking.** (partially-addressed) — As pharmacologic weight loss approaches surgical magnitudes across a very large future patient population, undetected sarcopenia could offset metabolic benefit and harm function in vulnerable groups. *Flagged by [linge2024].*
- **At current net prices, the most effective incretin therapies (tirzepatide, semaglutide) are not cost-effective, and the price reductions required for equitable access (30.5% and 81.9% respectively to reach $100k/QALY) have not been achieved.** (open) — Access and cost determine whether the demonstrated population-level health benefits are actually realized equitably rather than confined to those who can pay. *Flagged by [hwang2025].*
- **Long-term (multi-year) safety and tolerability of incretin agonists at obesity doses remains incompletely characterized; even in the largest outcomes trial, adverse-event-driven permanent discontinuation was roughly twice that of placebo (16.6% vs 8.2%).** (partially-addressed) — Chronic indefinite dosing in a broad, often non-diabetic population makes durable tolerability and rare long-term safety signals decisive for the benefit-risk balance. *Flagged by [lincoff2023].*
- **Comparative long-term efficacy and safety across the rapidly expanding class — dual vs triple agonists, injectable vs oral, peptide vs small-molecule — is largely established only on short-to-medium-term surrogate endpoints (weight, HbA1c) rather than head-to-head hard-outcome data.** (partially-addressed) — Clinicians and payers must choose among many agents; without head-to-head outcome trials, sequencing and selection rest on indirect comparison and surrogate endpoints. *Flagged by [aronne2025], [rosenstock2026], [jastreboff2023], [wharton2023].*
- **The relative mechanistic contributions to weight loss — central vs peripheral GLP-1 action, vagal vs central GLP1R+ neuronal pathways, and reduced food intake vs altered energy expenditure — are not fully resolved, and the added value of GIP and glucagon co-agonism over GLP-1 alone is still being mapped to clinical effect.** (partially-addressed) — Understanding which mechanisms drive efficacy guides rational design of next-generation single, dual, and triple agonists and predicts which combinations will translate to durable clinical benefit. *Flagged by [drucker2022], [coskun2018], [campbell2013].*

## Open debates / contested points

- **Does a dual GIP/GLP-1 agonist (tirzepatide) deliver clinically superior weight loss compared with a GLP-1 receptor agonist (semaglutide) in obesity without diabetes?**  *(resolved)*
    - *Tirzepatide is superior to semaglutide for weight loss.* ([aronne2025], [coskun2018], [jastreboff2022]): The head-to-head SURMOUNT-5 trial directly demonstrated greater weight reduction with maximum-tolerated-dose tirzepatide (-20.2%) than semaglutide 2.4 mg (-13.7%) at 72 weeks (P<0.001), and superiority for waist circumference, resolving the prior reliance on cross-trial comparisons. Preclinical work had already shown the dual agonist decreased weight more potently than a GLP-1 agonist in mice.
    - *Before the direct trial, the comparison was unresolved and rested on indirect evidence.* ([aronne2025], [jastreboff2022], [wilding2021]): Prior to SURMOUNT-5 the comparative efficacy and safety of tirzepatide versus semaglutide in adults with obesity without diabetes was explicitly unknown, with single-agent pivotal trials (SURMOUNT-1 ~20.9% at 15 mg; STEP 1 -14.9%) only comparable across separate trial populations.
- **Among oral GLP-1 options for cardiometabolic disease, does a small-molecule nonpeptide (orforglipron) or an oral peptide (oral semaglutide) offer the better benefit-risk profile?**
    - *Orforglipron offers greater glycemic efficacy.* ([rosenstock2026], [wharton2023]): In the ACHIEVE-3 head-to-head trial in type 2 diabetes, orforglipron 12 mg and 36 mg were non-inferior and superior to oral semaglutide 7 mg and 14 mg for HbA1c reduction at 52 weeks (treatment difference -0.48% for 12 mg vs 7 mg).
    - *The greater efficacy of orforglipron comes with a tolerability trade-off, and oral semaglutide already shows meaningful weight efficacy.* ([rosenstock2026], [knop2023]): ACHIEVE-3 identified higher rates of gastrointestinal adverse events with orforglipron than oral semaglutide, while high-dose oral semaglutide 50 mg produced superior, clinically meaningful weight loss vs placebo in OASIS 1 — leaving the optimal oral choice unresolved across glycemic vs weight vs tolerability endpoints.
