Current evidence on improving sleep and treating insomnia in adults converges on one clear first-line treatment, with most consumer interventions ranging from modestly supported to overhyped.

**CBT-I is first-line and efficacious.** Cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia—combining stimulus control, sleep restriction, cognitive restructuring, sleep hygiene, and relaxation—is the strong initial recommendation in the ACP clinical practice guideline (10.7326/m15-2175), with pharmacotherapy added only via shared decision-making if CBT-I fails. A meta-analysis of 20 RCTs in 1,162 adults (Trauer et al., 10.7326/m14-2841) found face-to-face CBT-I improved sleep onset latency by ~19 min, wake after sleep onset by ~26 min, and sleep efficiency by ~9.9 points. A larger 87-RCT meta-analysis (van Straten et al., 10.1016/j.smrv.2017.02.001) reported a large effect on insomnia severity (g = 0.98) but only a small effect on total sleep time (g = 0.16). Internet-delivered CBT-I yields comparable effects (g = 0.21–1.09) maintained at 4–48 weeks (Zachariae et al., 10.1016/j.smrv.2015.10.004), though scaling access remains unsolved.

**Supplements: modest and unreliable.** A 19-RCT melatonin meta-analysis (Ferracioli-Oda et al., 10.1371/journal.pone.0063773) found only ~7 min faster sleep latency and ~8 min more total sleep. A dosing RCT (Zhdanova et al., 10.1210/jcem.86.10.7901) showed a low physiologic 0.3 mg dose taken 30 min before bed restored sleep efficiency, while 3.0 mg left melatonin elevated into daytime and induced hypothermia—favoring low-dose timing. Critically, commercial supplements ranged from −83% to +478% of label and often contained serotonin (Erland & Saxena, 10.5664/jcsm.6462), so even modest benefits may not be reliably delivered. The AASM guideline recommends against melatonin for chronic insomnia (10.5664/jcsm.6470). Magnesium reduced sleep latency by ~17 min across 3 small RCTs, but on low-to-very-low quality evidence with high bias risk (Mah et al., 10.1186/s12906-021-03297-z).

**Light/circadian.** Light therapy produced small but significant gains in sleep continuity (ES = −0.23) and advanced delayed sleep timing in intrinsic circadian disorders (Faulkner et al., 10.1016/j.smrv.2019.04.012).

**Hype vs. evidence.** Blue-light-blocking glasses showed only a non-significant latency change in pooled crossover trials (Luna-Rangel et al., 10.3389/fneur.2025.1699303); the melatonin-timing mechanism is real (Liset et al., 10.1016/j.nbscr.2021.100074) but objective benefits are not. Mouth-taping rests on a handful of small studies (only 2 of 10 positive) with a serious asphyxiation harm signal (Rhee et al., 10.1371/journal.pone.0323643). Trackers overestimate sleep and misclassify wake (specificity 0.35–0.45; Chinoy et al., 10.2147/nss.s348795; 39–45% in insomnia, Kahawage et al., 10.1111/jsr.12931), are not diagnostic substitutes (Agostinho et al., 10.1080/15402002.2026.2673893), and can drive "orthosomnia"—perfectionistic sleep anxiety (Baron et al., 10.5664/jcsm.6472).
