## Improving Sleep and Treating Insomnia in Adults: An Evidence Review

**First-line treatment.** Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is endorsed as first-line therapy ahead of pharmacotherapy by the American College of Physicians (10.7326/M15-2175, Qaseem 2016) and the American Academy of Sleep Medicine. Its core components are stimulus control, sleep restriction (sleep consolidation), cognitive restructuring, sleep hygiene, and relaxation training. A large meta-analysis of multicomponent CBT-I (10.7326/M14-2841, Trauer 2015) found clinically meaningful improvements in sleep onset latency, wake after sleep onset, and sleep efficiency that are durable. Digital CBT-I (e.g., Sleepio) shows efficacy in RCTs such as Espie's work (10.5665/sleep.6072) and a large effectiveness trial (10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2018.2745, Freeman 2017, Lancet Psychiatry mediation of mental health). Effect sizes are robust but access and therapist availability remain limiting.

**Supplements.** Melatonin acts as a chronobiotic more than a hypnotic; meta-analyses (10.1371/journal.pone.0063773, Ferracioli-Oda 2013) show modest reductions in sleep onset latency (~7 min) and small increases in total sleep time. Crucially, low physiologic doses (0.3-0.5 mg) timed several hours before desired sleep for circadian phase-advance often outperform high doses (3-10 mg); over-the-counter products are frequently mislabeled (10.5664/jcsm.6462, Erland & Saxena 2017, found 26% variance from label). Magnesium evidence is weak; a systematic review (10.1186/s12906-021-03297-z) found low-quality trials and insufficient evidence for routine supplementation.

**Light/circadian approaches.** Morning bright light and properly timed melatonin are the best-supported tools for circadian rhythm disorders and shift work; evening light/blue suppression of melatonin is well documented (10.1210/jc.2010-2098, Gooley). Limiting evening light helps phase, but blue-light-blocking glasses have inconsistent RCT support and a Cochrane review (10.1002/14651858.CD013244.pub2, Singh 2023) found no clear benefit for sleep or eyestrain.

**Contested/hype interventions.** Mouth-taping for mouth breathing/OSA lacks quality evidence and carries aspiration/safety concerns; small studies are inconclusive and it can be dangerous in undiagnosed obstructive apnea. Consumer wearable sleep trackers have limited accuracy versus polysomnography, especially for staging, and can paradoxically worsen sleep through preoccupation - "orthosomnia" (10.5664/jcsm.6472, Baron 2017). The honest summary: CBT-I and timed light/low-dose melatonin are genuinely evidence-supported; magnesium, blue-blockers, mouth-taping, and tracker-driven optimization are largely hype with thin or contested evidence.
