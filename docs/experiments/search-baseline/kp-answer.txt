Our work builds on Rubrics as Rewards (RaR), which first showed that instance-specific structured rubrics graded by an LLM judge can serve as the on-policy RL reward beyond verifiable domains, yielding up to +31% relative improvement on HealthBench over direct Likert-based LLM-as-judge rewards and better alignment for smaller judges (arXiv:2507.17746). A 2026 wave of named systems has since attacked RaR's two central weaknesses — lossy scalar aggregation and static rubric authoring. On the aggregation side, SRaR attributes each rubric item to a specific reasoning step and normalizes per-step scores across rollouts, arguing that response-level scalarization loses multi-criterion structure and uniformly supervises correct and incorrect steps (arXiv:2605.17291); POW3R reweights criteria by their current usefulness as an optimization signal rather than their human-assigned importance, winning 24 of 30 comparisons against static aggregation (arXiv:2605.20164); Open Rubric System replaces the learned pointwise judge with pairwise adaptive rubrics executed as an inspectable reasoning process (arXiv:2602.14069); RUBRIC-ARROW alternates pointwise rubric reward modeling with policy updates (arXiv:2605.29156); and RubricEM treats rubrics as a shared interface structuring policy execution, judge feedback, and agent memory in a meta-RL loop (arXiv:2605.10899). On the rubric-generation side — closest to our contribution — EvoRubric self-evolves rubrics for open-ended generation and shows compatibility with expert priors (arXiv:2605.29847), RLCER self-proposes and evolves rubrics to supervise chain-of-thought without outcome rewards (arXiv:2602.10885), AMARIS adds a global training memory so rubric edits are informed by recurring failures rather than local batch evidence (arXiv:2605.18592), QUBRIC co-designs queries with rubrics, arguing rubric quality is bottlenecked by query structure (arXiv:2606.03968), and Shen et al. diagnose why rubric generation remains hard to control (coverage gaps, conflated dimensions, correlated criteria) (arXiv:2602.05125). We take seriously the failure-mode literature these systems must survive: Mahmoud et al. show weak verifiers produce proxy-reward gains that do not transfer and that stronger verification reduces but does not eliminate exploitation (arXiv:2605.12474); Wang et al. reproduce and detect subtle, bias-entangled hacking in rubric RL (arXiv:2606.04923); Pombal et al. show self-preference bias persists even under objective rubrics and survives judge ensembling (arXiv:2604.06996) — a direct threat to self-evolving loops where policy and judge share a backbone; and Ackermann et al. argue the durable fix is optimization-side gradient regularization rather than better rubrics alone (arXiv:2602.18037). Our method sits at the intersection of these threads: a self-evolving rubric system that adopts step-aware, saturation-aware aggregation while incorporating optimization-side safeguards, rather than treating rubric quality and hacking resistance as separable problems.

Open problems our method addresses:

1. Reward hacking in self-evolving loops is unsolved. Exploitation of latent judge biases grows over training and survives stronger verification (arXiv:2605.12474, arXiv:2606.04923), and self-preference bias contaminates rubric rewards even with fully objective criteria, with ensembling only mitigating it (arXiv:2604.06996). Existing self-evolving systems (arXiv:2605.29847, arXiv:2602.10885) rely on rubric-side verification pipelines, while Ackermann et al. (arXiv:2602.18037) show optimization-side regularization is needed; no system yet combines both, which is precisely the gap a trustworthy self-evolving method must close.

2. Principled aggregation of multi-criterion rubric signals is missing. Static scalarization conflates human-assigned importance with current training usefulness (arXiv:2605.20164), loses multi-criterion structure and mis-rewards individual steps (arXiv:2605.17291), and creates the information bottleneck that invites hacking (arXiv:2602.14069, arXiv:2605.29156). A self-evolving system compounds this: as rubrics change, any fixed aggregation rule goes stale, so evolution and aggregation must be co-designed.

3. There is no standardized protocol for validating that rubric rewards track true human-judged quality. Methods are validated against heterogeneous benchmarks, judge panels, and self-defined diagnostics (arXiv:2605.12474, arXiv:2507.17746, arXiv:2602.14069), and cross-domain transfer of rubric-trained policies is only partially demonstrated (arXiv:2606.03968, arXiv:2605.29847, arXiv:2605.18592). For self-evolving rubrics the stakes are higher: without an external fidelity check, an evolving rubric can drift toward what its own judge rewards, so reported gains may be artifacts of the evolving reward itself.
